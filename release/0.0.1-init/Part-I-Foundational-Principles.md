# Part I: Foundational Principles & Legal Modalities

This section establishes the immutable legal baselines for software architecture, grounding all digital automated systems within existing international human rights instruments.

## Article 1: The Primacy of Human Rights Instruments in Digital Spaces

1.1. **Universal Application:** All rights, freedoms, and protections established under international human rights law apply fully and without exception within all digital, computational, and networked environments.
1.2. **Architectural Compliance:** The architecture of software, algorithms, and computational models shall not be designed or deployed in a manner that supersedes, bypasses, or diminishes established human rights.
1.3. **Interpretation:** In any conflict between the operational logic of a software system and human rights instruments, the latter shall maintain absolute legal and operational primacy.

## Article 2: The Strict Status of Automation as an Extension of Human Agency

2.1. **Instrumental Nature:** All software systems, algorithms, and automated processes—regardless of their complexity or capacity for inference—are strictly tools and instruments. They possess no independent agency.
2.2. **Human Locus:** Every action executed by an automated system is fundamentally an extension of the agency of the human actor(s) who designed, deployed, or directed the system. 
2.3. **Accountability:** The delegation of tasks to automated systems does not absolve the delegating human entity of accountability or legal liability for the outcomes of those tasks.

## Article 3: Absolute Rejection of Legal Personhood or Moral Agency for Code

3.1. **No Legal Personhood:** No software, algorithm, model, or artificial intelligence system shall be granted legal personhood, moral agency, or any form of independent standing under the law.
3.2. **Prohibition of Shifting Liability:** Legal responsibility cannot be transferred to, or assumed by, any non-human computational entity. Attempting to attribute independent intent or fault to a software system in order to shield human actors from liability is explicitly prohibited.

## Article 4: Inviolability of the Individual's Cognitive and Expressive Outputs

4.1. **Protection of Outputs:** The cognitive, creative, and expressive outputs of human beings are inviolable and remain inherently bound to the human author.
4.2. **Consent and Context:** The automated ingestion, processing, or replication of an individual's cognitive outputs by a computational model requires explicit, verifiable consent, and must respect the context in which the output was originally produced.
4.3. **Non-Extraction:** Software systems must not be used to strip human expression of its authorship or transform personal cognitive labor into anonymous public utility without authorization.

## Article 5: The Right to Cryptographic Self-Determination and Verification

5.1. **Cryptographic Identity:** Individuals possess the right to assert and control their digital presence and identity through independent, decentralized cryptographic means, rather than relying solely on centralized providers.
5.2. **Right of Verification:** Every individual has the right to cryptographically verify the integrity, origin, and modification history of data that pertains to them or their outputs.
5.3. **Non-Repudiation:** The use of cryptographic signatures to assert human agency or consent shall be legally recognized and protected against arbitrary repudiation.

## Article 6: The Structural Decoupling of Legal Authorship from Mechanical Execution

6.1. **Clear Boundaries:** There must be a structurally enforced distinction between human-authored intent and the mechanical execution, generation, or inference performed by a software system.
6.2. **Attribution of Automation:** Any digital artifact, text, code, or decision that is generated entirely or substantially through automated inference must be explicitly identifiable as a mechanical execution, rather than being conflated with direct legal human authorship.
6.3. **Co-Signing Protocol:** When human intent and machine execution are blended, the exact scope of human intervention must be cryptographically or structurally verifiable, ensuring that legal authorship is only claimed for the human-contributed portion.

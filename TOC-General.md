## International Charter for Human-Centric AI Engineering and Governance

### Preamble

*Establishing the technological environment as a vital modality for exercising fundamental human rights, rejecting machine personhood, and asserting that all computational tools must operate strictly as instruments under human direction and accountability.*

### Part I: Foundational Principles & Legal Modalities

This section establishes the immutable legal baselines for software architecture, grounding all digital automated systems within existing international human rights instruments.

* **Article 1:** The Primacy of Human Rights Instruments in Digital Spaces
* **Article 2:** The Strict Status of Automation as an Extension of Human Agency
* **Article 3:** Absolute Rejection of Legal Personhood or Moral Agency for Code
* **Article 4:** Inviolability of the Individual's Cognitive and Expressive Outputs
* **Article 5:** The Right to Cryptographic Self-Determination and Verification
* **Article 6:** The Structural Decoupling of Legal Authorship from Mechanical Execution

### Part II: System Typologies & Agent Boundaries

This section defines the objective boundaries between human actors and varying classes of software to prevent the obfuscation of responsibility.

* **Article 7:** The Human Actor as the Sole Locus of Accountability and Rights
* **Article 8:** Classification of Deterministic Agents (Procedural and Rule-Based Software)
* **Article 9:** Classification of Cognitive Agents (Probabilistic and Inference-Based Systems)
* **Article 10:** Classification of Broker Agents (Interfaces, Connection Protocols, and Orchestrators)
* **Article 11:** Differentiation of Edge-Localized Processing vs. Centralized Hosted Environments
* **Article 12:** Inter-Agent Communication Standards and Boundary Isolation Protocols

### Part III: The Enumerated State & Verifiable Presence

This section eliminates centralized tracking and abstract definitions of digital presence, replacing them with a decentralized mathematical framework.

* **Article 13:** Rejection of Centralized and Single-Provider Identity Systems
* **Article 14:** Definition of Presence via an Enumerated State
* **Article 15:** Utilization of Multiple Cryptography-Supported Identifiers
* **Article 16:** Architecture of Agent-Centric Datasets (Data tied to the executing mechanism)
* **Article 17:** Architecture of Entity-Centric Datasets (Data tied to the human or asset)
* **Article 18:** Protocols for Cryptographic Ephemerality and Temporal Credentialing

### Part IV: Operational Provenance & The Agent Header Protocol

This section outlines the exact engineering requirements for tracking machine intervention in data and source code without introducing legal ambiguities.

* **Article 19:** The Modality of Machine Actuation (Clear boundaries for autonomous execution)
* **Article 20:** The Mandate for Continuous, Event-Driven Provenance Tracking
* **Article 21:** Structural Requirements for the Agent Header Telemetry Payload
* **Article 22:** Mandatory Telemetry Elements (Model specifications, operational parameters, timestamps)
* **Article 23:** Mechanics for Human Cryptographic Co-Signing of Automated Changes
* **Article 24:** Prohibition of Opaque Machine Modifications and Automated Content Ingestion

### Part V: Distributed Architecture & Context Preservation

This section focuses on preventing the monopolization of context and ensuring that connection interfaces function as open, neutral protocols.

* **Article 25:** Open Connection Interfaces as Stateless Intermediaries
* **Article 26:** The Architectural Separation of Compute Power from Data Repositories
* **Article 27:** Enforcing the Principle of Bringing the Model to the Data
* **Article 28:** Mitigating Walled-Garden Monopolies Through Universal Interoperability
* **Article 29:** Portability of the Enumerated State Across Disconnected Environments
* **Article 30:** Classification of Computational Inference as an Interchangeable, Public Utility

### Part VI: Graph Topologies & Distributed Data Encoding

This section mandates the shift away from volatile text-based context windows toward structured, relational, and mathematically verifiable data webs.

* **Article 31:** Structural Requirements for Graph-Based Knowledge Models
* **Article 32:** Encoding Architectural Rules into Foundational Data Relatables
* **Article 33:** Semantic Mapping of System Relations and Operational Clearances
* **Article 34:** Bidirectional Cryptographic Attestation Protocols for External Network Nodes
* **Article 35:** Scoping and Isolation of Subgraphs for Targeted Machine Processing
* **Article 36:** Synchronous and Asynchronous State Reconciliation via Delta-Tracking

### Part VII: Perimeter Enforcement, Compliance, & Technical Debt Mitigation

This section details how systems must programmatically defend themselves against non-compliant tools, ensuring long-term systemic stability.

* **Article 37:** Automated Transaction Rejection for Non-Attested Telemetry
* **Article 38:** Quarantine and Remediation Protocols for Malformed Agent Headers
* **Article 39:** Boundary Resolution Mechanisms for Cryptographic Attribution Failures
* **Article 40:** Independent Auditing Modalities for Verifying Closed-Box Model Compliance
* **Article 41:** Containment Strategies for MVP-Stage Technical Debt in Open Protocols
* **Article 42:** Mechanisms for Collective Ratification, Local Forking, and Version Evolution

# Part VI: Graph Topologies & Distributed Data Encoding

This section mandates the shift away from volatile text-based context windows toward structured, relational, and mathematically verifiable data webs.

## Article 31: Structural Requirements for Graph-Based Knowledge Models

31.1. **Graph Foundations:** To ensure transparency and interoperability, complex system state, rulesets, and context must be mapped using graph-based knowledge models (such as RDF, OWL, or decentralized graph equivalents) rather than relying solely on unstructured, embedded text vectors.
31.2. **Explicit Relationships:** Relationships between data entities, human actors, and software agents must be encoded as explicit, queryable edges within the graph, preventing AI models from inferring relationships that have not been structurally defined and authorized.
31.3. **Human Readability:** While optimized for machine parsing, the foundational schemas of the graph must remain fundamentally translatable into human-readable formats to ensure meaningful oversight and auditing.

## Article 32: Encoding Architectural Rules into Foundational Data Relatables

32.1. **Data-Centric Rules:** Permissions, operational boundaries, and system rules must be encoded directly into the foundational data relationships (the ontology) rather than being hardcoded entirely within the application layer or model weights.
32.2. **Self-Describing Data:** Data must be self-describing, carrying its own usage restrictions and cryptographic signatures, ensuring that the rules govern the data regardless of which agent or interface is accessing it.
32.3. **Ontological Sovereignty:** Communities and individuals maintain the right to define and govern the ontologies that structure their data, resisting attempts by centralized platforms to impose universal, flattened data schemas that erase cultural or contextual nuance.

## Article 33: Semantic Mapping of System Relations and Operational Clearances

33.1. **Clearance Mapping:** The operational clearance of any automated agent (what it is allowed to read, write, or execute) must be semantically mapped within the graph topology, linking the agent's identity directly to specific nodes and authorized actions.
33.2. **Dynamic Context Resolution:** As context changes, semantic mappings must allow for dynamic resolution of permissions, immediately revoking an agent's access if the underlying human authorization or context shifts within the graph.
33.3. **Prevention of Inference Bypass:** Agents are strictly prohibited from using probabilistic inference to bypass semantic mappings or deduce restricted information that is explicitly walled off within the graph topology.

## Article 34: Bidirectional Cryptographic Attestation Protocols for External Network Nodes

34.1. **Mutual Verification:** When two external nodes or graph ecosystems connect, they must employ bidirectional cryptographic attestation. Both the querying agent and the serving node must mathematically prove their identity, integrity, and adherence to shared protocols before data exchange.
34.2. **Zero-Trust Baseline:** Networks must operate on a zero-trust baseline, where no internal or external agent is implicitly trusted. Every transaction must be validated against the semantic permissions mapped in the graph.
34.3. **Attestation Logs:** The cryptographic proofs exchanged during bidirectional attestation must be logged within the Agent Header, providing a permanent, auditable record of network interactions.

## Article 35: Scoping and Isolation of Subgraphs for Targeted Machine Processing

35.1. **Need-to-Know Processing:** When delegating a task to a Cognitive Agent, human operators must isolate and scope a specific "subgraph" of data necessary for the task, rather than granting the agent unbounded access to the entire Entity-Centric dataset.
35.2. **Architectural Quarantine:** These subgraphs must act as architectural quarantines, ensuring that even if the agent acts unpredictably, the potential for data corruption or unauthorized exfiltration is structurally contained.
35.3. **Ephemeral Context Windows:** Once the agent completes its assigned task, its access to the scoped subgraph must be mathematically severed, enforcing the principle of ephemeral context windows.

## Article 36: Synchronous and Asynchronous State Reconciliation via Delta-Tracking

36.1. **Delta-Tracking Systems:** Distributed systems must maintain state consensus not through massive, centralized data overrides, but through precise, cryptographic "delta-tracking"—recording only the specific, verified changes (deltas) made to the graph.
36.2. **Asynchronous Autonomy:** Systems must support asynchronous state reconciliation, allowing edge devices and localized nodes to operate autonomously while offline and seamlessly resolve their delta-changes with the broader network upon reconnection.
36.3. **Conflict Resolution:** In cases of conflicting state updates, resolution mechanisms must prioritize human-signed deltas over agent-generated deltas, reinforcing the primacy of human agency in all digital environments.
